    About This Visualization

    Motivation

    California's wildfire crisis has intensified dramatically over the past two decades, driven by prolonged drought, rising temperatures, and expanded development in fire-prone landscapes. This visualization enables interactive exploration of how fire activity has evolved spatially and temporally across the state, using two complementary data sources: NASA's MODIS Terra satellite (via the GIBS WMTS API) and historical fire perimeter records from CAL FIRE's Fire and Resource Assessment Program (FRAP).

    Design Rationale

    Satellite tile overlay: Rather than abstracting fire locations into dots on a blank basemap, we fetch actual MODIS satellite imagery tiles from the NASA GIBS REST API (WMTS, EPSG:4326) and draw them onto an HTML canvas element using D3's geoEquirectangular projection. This lets viewers see smoke plumes, burn scars, and active fire fronts in their real geographic context. A D3 SVG layer sits on top for county boundaries and interaction targets.

    Two-layer compositing: When a fire detection layer is selected, the map renders the TrueColor basemap first (JPEG, opaque), then composites the thermal anomaly layer on top (PNG with transparent background) — so only fire hotspot pixels are visible over the satellite imagery. The opacity slider controls how strongly the fire layer appears.

    Time slider: Temporal change is the central story. A static map can show accumulated fire history but not when or how rapidly conditions evolve. The slider lets viewers scrub month-by-month across 2000–2024, exposing seasonal patterns (peak activity: July–October) and year-over-year escalation. Animated playback runs the timeline forward automatically.

    County click + sidebar: Satellite imagery conveys location and extent but not magnitude. Pairing the map with a statewide acreage bar chart and county-level statistics grounds visual impressions in concrete numbers and enables regional comparison that would be impossible in a static view.

    Visual design: A dark background with amber/orange accents reinforces the fire theme while maximizing contrast against the satellite imagery. Bebas Neue display type and Space Mono monospace give the interface a data-instrument character appropriate to a scientific monitoring tool.

    Data Transformations

    The CAL FIRE FRAP fire perimeters dataset was filtered to California fires from 2000–2024. Records were aggregated in two ways: by year (total acres burned statewide, for the bar chart) and by county (fire count, total acres, largest single fire name and acreage, worst year by acreage). GIBS WMTS tiles are requested at 1 km resolution for fire detection layers and 250 m for TrueColor, using monthly date steps; the date parameter updates on every slider change. Occasional cloud cover may obscure imagery on individual days — comparing nearby months typically resolves this.
